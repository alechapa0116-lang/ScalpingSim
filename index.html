SCALPING ACADEMY

Everything you need to know to trade futures like a professional scalper

What is Scalping?

Scalping is a trading style where you make many small trades throughout the day, holding each one for only seconds to a few minutes. You're not trying to catch a big move — you're grabbing tiny profits over and over.

Imagine a poker player who wins $5 on most hands instead of waiting for one huge pot. The small wins add up fast with enough volume.

A professional scalper might make 30–150 trades per day, each targeting just 2–6 ticks of profit. That's $25–$75 per trade on ES. Do that 50 times and you've made $1,250–$3,750 in a single day.

Why Scalp Futures?

Speed — Futures react to news before stocks do. You can be in and out before most people even see the candle form.

Leverage — Control a $250,000 position with $500 of margin. This magnifies tiny moves into real money.

Liquidity — ES (S&P 500 futures) trades millions of contracts daily. You can enter and exit instantly at any size without moving the market.

24hr market — Futures trade nearly around the clock. You can scalp at 3am if you want.

ES trades roughly $150 billion in notional value every single day — more than most stocks combined.

The Hard Truth

Scalping is one of the hardest trading styles to master. Most retail traders lose money scalping because:

• The spread and commissions eat into tiny profits

• Emotions cause you to hold losers too long

• Overtrading — taking bad setups out of boredom

• Not having a clear "wrong" level to exit at

The good news: this is a simulator. Blow up your account 100 times here until you get it right — that's the entire point.

Scalping vs Other Styles

Scalping — Seconds to minutes. 2–6 tick target. Many trades/day.

Day Trading — Minutes to hours. Bigger moves. Flat by close.

Swing Trading — Days to weeks. Big moves. Holds overnight.

Investing — Years. Doesn't care about daily noise.

Most traders on YouTube are swing traders or investors. True scalping at the professional level looks very different — fast, disciplined, mechanical.

What is a Futures Contract?

A futures contract is an agreement to buy or sell an asset at a specific price on a specific future date. You don't own the asset — you own the contract.

Like putting a deposit on a house that's being built. You lock in today's price to buy it in 3 months. If prices go up, your contract is worth more. If they go down, it's worth less.

As a scalper you almost never hold to expiration — you're in and out in seconds, just profiting from the price movement of the contract itself.

ES — The King of Scalping

The E-mini S&P 500 (ES) is the most traded futures contract in the world. It tracks the S&P 500 index.

Tick size: 0.25 points = $12.50 per tick

1 point = 4 ticks = $50.00

Margin: ~$500 per contract (varies by broker)

So if ES moves 4 ticks (1 full point) in your favor, you make $50. On 5 contracts that's $250 in seconds.

On a normal day ES moves 20–60 points. Scalpers try to capture 2–4 point moves repeatedly throughout the session.

NQ — High Octane

The E-mini Nasdaq (NQ) tracks the Nasdaq 100. It's more volatile than ES — bigger moves, bigger risk.

Tick size: 0.25 points = $5.00 per tick

1 point = 4 ticks = $20.00

NQ can move 100+ points in minutes during major news. Great for scalping big tech earnings. More experienced scalpers prefer NQ for larger swings.

When Apple or NVIDIA releases earnings, NQ can spike or drop 200 points in seconds. That's $4,000 per contract.

Other Instruments

CL (Crude Oil) — Very volatile. Reacts to geopolitical news, supply data. $10/tick. Pros love it.

GC (Gold) — Safe haven. Spikes during market fear or dollar weakness. $10/tick.

BTC Futures — Extreme volatility. Trades 24/7. High risk, high reward.

EUR/USD — Forex pair. Moves in pips. Most liquid currency pair in the world.

Most beginner scalpers start with ES because it's the most predictable and has the most educational resources available.
Tick
The smallest possible price movement for a futures contract.
ES tick = 0.25 points = $12.50. Move 4 ticks = $50.
Point
Equal to 4 ticks. A common way pros talk about moves.
"I made 3 points on that trade" = 12 ticks = $150 on ES.
Bid
The highest price a buyer is willing to pay right now.
If the bid is 5000.00, someone will buy from you at that price instantly.
Ask
The lowest price a seller will accept right now.
If the ask is 5000.25, you can buy at that price right now.
Spread
The gap between bid and ask. The cost of entry.
Bid 5000.00 / Ask 5000.25 = 1 tick spread = $12.50 cost just to get in.
DOM
Depth of Market. Shows all pending buy and sell orders at each price level.
Big numbers at a price = a "wall." Price often stalls or bounces off walls.
Long
You bought a contract. You profit if price goes UP.
"I'm long 2 ES" = I bought 2 contracts, hoping the S&P goes up.
Short
You sold a contract you don't own. You profit if price goes DOWN.
"I'm short 1 NQ" = I sold 1 Nasdaq contract, hoping it drops.
Flat
No open position. You're out of the market entirely.
Pros say "get flat" when they want to close everything immediately.
Fill
Your order was executed at the exchange.
"I got filled at 5001.50" = my buy order executed at that price.
Slippage
Getting filled at a worse price than expected during fast markets.
You tried to buy at 5000.25 but the market moved fast and you got filled at 5000.75. That's 2 ticks of slippage.
Mark to Market
Your P&L updates live every tick while you hold a position.
You're long 1 ES at 5000. It moves to 5002 = +$100 unrealized. It's not yours until you close.
Stop Loss
An automatic exit order triggered if price moves against you by X ticks.
Long ES at 5000 with a 4-tick stop = auto-exit at 4999.00. Max loss = $50.
Target
Your planned exit price for profit. Set before entering.
"My target is 4 ticks" = I'll close when price moves 4 ticks in my favor.
Margin
The deposit required to hold a futures contract. Much less than the contract's value.
ES is worth ~$250,000 but only requires ~$500 margin. That's 500:1 leverage.
Leverage
Controlling a large position with a small deposit. Amplifies gains AND losses.
$500 controlling $250,000 = any 1% move in the asset = 500% move on your margin.
Drawdown
How far your account dropped from its highest point.
Account peaked at $30,000 then dropped to $27,000 = 10% drawdown. Most prop firms cut you off at 5-10%.
RTH
Regular Trading Hours. 9:30am–4:00pm EST for US futures.
Most volume and best setups happen during RTH. Globex (overnight) is thinner and trickier.
Prop Firm
A company that gives you their capital to trade after you pass an evaluation.
Topstep, Apex Trader Funding, FTMO. Pass the eval → trade $50K–$200K → keep 70-90% of profits.
Funded Account
A prop firm account where you trade the firm's money, not your own.
The dream for most retail traders. You risk none of your own money but share in the profits.
Tick Value
How many dollars one tick movement is worth.
ES = $12.50/tick. NQ = $5/tick. CL = $10/tick. GC = $10/tick.
Session P&L
Total profit or loss for the current trading day only.
Most scalpers set a daily profit target (e.g. +$500) and a loss limit (e.g. -$200). Hit either = stop trading.

The DOM — Your Most Important Tool

The Depth of Market (DOM) — also called the order book or ladder — is a live list of every pending buy and sell order at every price level. It's the single most important tool for a futures scalper.

The DOM has three columns:

Left (Ask volume — red) — The number of contracts people want to SELL at each price. These are your sellers. To go long (buy), you need to get through the ask side.

Center (Price) — The price level. Click it to enter a trade at that price. Prices update every fraction of a second.

Right (Bid volume — green) — The number of contracts people want to BUY at each price. These are your buyers. To go short (sell), you need to get through the bid side.

Imagine an auction. The DOM shows you every hand raised to bid, and every seller shouting their price. You can see the whole room at once.

Reading the DOM

Large number at a price = a "wall" or "iceberg." Price tends to stall or reverse here because there's a lot of supply or demand to work through.

Numbers disappearing fast = orders being pulled. Someone big was protecting that level but just left — could mean a breakout is coming.

Price prints = trades that actually happened. The DOM shows pending orders; actual trades are shown in the "Time & Sales" tape.

If you see 2,000 contracts sitting at the ask of 5000.00 and price keeps bouncing off it, that's a huge sell wall. Don't try to break through it — trade with it by selling the bounces.

Using the DOM to Trade

In this simulator, the DOM shows 20 price levels above and below the current price, updating live every tick.

• Click any price row to enter a limit order at that price

• Hit the big green BUY button to buy at the current ask instantly

• Hit the big red SELL button to sell at the current bid instantly

• Hit FLAT to close your entire position immediately at market

Watch the DOM while in a trade — if big ask volume appears above you while long, consider exiting before it blocks your move.

1

Pick ONE instrument and master it

Start with ES. Watch it for days before trading. Learn its rhythm — how fast it moves, when it speeds up (market open, news), when it slows down (lunch hour). Most professional scalpers only trade 1-2 instruments their entire career.

2

Identify your key levels BEFORE the session

Before you trade, mark the previous day's high and low, the overnight high and low, and round numbers (5000, 5050, 5100 on ES). Price reacts at these levels because everyone can see them. These are your roadmap.

3

Watch for price approaching a level

Don't just randomly click buy and sell. Wait for price to approach one of your levels. At that level, watch the DOM — does big bid volume appear? Do sellers dry up? Does price start consolidating (bouncing in a tight range)? These are entry signals.

4

Define your stop BEFORE entering

Before you click buy or sell, decide: "If price goes X ticks against me, I'm wrong and I get out." Set your stop. 4-6 ticks is standard for ES scalping. This keeps one bad trade from destroying your day. No stop = gambling, not trading.

5

Enter with conviction — one click

When your setup is there, click immediately. Hesitation costs ticks. Hit the BUY or SELL button, not a limit order in a fast market. You can always miss a tick getting in, but a missed entry means no trade at all.

6

Manage the trade — don't watch P&L

While in a trade, watch PRICE — not your dollar P&L. The P&L number will mess with your emotions. Focus on whether price is moving toward your target or toward your stop. Make your decision based on price action, not fear or greed.

7

Exit clean — take your target or your stop

When price hits your target, click FLAT immediately. Don't get greedy and let it run — scalping is about consistent small wins. If your stop hits, accept it and move on. One loss means nothing if you have a positive expectancy over 100 trades.

8

Review every trade

After your session, look at your log. Why did you win? Why did you lose? Were you following your rules? Most professionals journal every trade. Patterns emerge — you'll notice you always lose after a big winner, or you always nail the open. Use that data.

✅

Always know your stop before entering

If you don't know where you're wrong before you click, you're gambling. Every single professional scalper has a defined maximum loss per trade. Usually 4–8 ticks on ES. That's $50–$100 max risk per trade.

✅

Trade with the trend on the 1-minute chart

Look at the 1-minute candles. Are they making higher highs and higher lows? That's an uptrend — only take long setups. Making lower lows? Only short. Fighting the trend is the fastest way to lose money scalping.

✅

Trade the open (9:30–11:30 EST) and close (3–4 EST)

These are the highest-volume periods. More volume = cleaner price action = easier setups. The lunch hour (12–2 EST) is the graveyard of scalpers — choppy, random, no follow-through. Most pros simply stop trading at 11:30 and come back at 3pm.

✅

Set a daily loss limit and respect it

Decide before you start: "If I lose $X today, I stop trading." Most prop firms enforce 5% daily drawdown. Even without one, set your own. Three losing trades in a row means something is off — step away. You can always come back tomorrow.

❌

Never hold through major news events

Fed rate decisions, CPI, Non-Farm Payrolls — these move markets 20–50+ points in milliseconds. No scalper can react fast enough. Get flat 5 minutes before any major event. The move after will be violent and unreliable. Let it settle, then re-enter.

❌

Never revenge trade

You took a loss. Now you want to "make it back" by immediately taking a bigger trade. This is revenge trading and it kills accounts. The market doesn't know you lost. It doesn't owe you anything. Take a break, reset, come back neutral.

❌

Never move your stop loss further away

Your stop is where you're wrong. Moving it further when price approaches it is just delaying the loss while making it bigger. This is how $50 losses turn into $500 losses. Set it. Honor it. No exceptions.

⚡

Watch round numbers — they're magnetic

ES at 5000, 5050, 5100. NQ at 18000, 18500. These numbers attract price like magnets because every trader on Earth has them marked. Entries near round numbers that hold have extremely high win rates.

⚡

Size up only after proving consistency

Start with 1 contract. Be profitable over 50 trades before adding a second. Be profitable over 100 trades before adding a third. Most failing traders try to trade 10 contracts before they've proven they can make money with 1. The size doesn't change the skill — it just changes the emotional pressure.

⚡

Your only job is process, not P&L

Focus on taking good setups with defined risk. The money is a byproduct of doing the right thing consistently. Traders who focus on P&L make emotional decisions. Traders who focus on process make mechanical ones. Mechanical wins over time.
